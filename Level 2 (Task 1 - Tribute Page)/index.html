A. P. J. Abdul Kalam

Missile Man of India

A.P.J. Abdul Kalam (born October 15, 1931, Rameswaram, India—died July 27, 2015, Shillong) Indian scientist and politician who played a leading role in the development of India’s missile and nuclear weapons programs. He was president of India from 2002 to 2007. Kalam earned a degree in aeronautical engineering from the Madras Institute of Technology and in 1958 joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO). In 1969 he moved to the Indian Space Research Organisation, where he was project director of the SLV-III, the first satellite launch vehicle that was both designed and produced in India. Rejoining DRDO in 1982, Kalam planned the program that produced a number of successful missiles, which helped earn him the nickname “Missile Man.” Among those successes was Agni, India’s first intermediate-range ballistic missile, which incorporated aspects of the SLV-III and was launched in 1989.

Biography of A. P. J. Abdul Kalam

  • Early Life
  • Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, India.
  • Belonged to a humble family, his father was a boat owner and his mother a housewife.
  • Education
  • Graduated in Physics from St. Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli.
  • Obtained an aerospace engineering degree from the Madras Institute of Technology.
  • Career
  • Joined the Defense Research and Development Organization (DRDO) in 1958.
  • Played a pivotal role in India's missile development program, notably in the development of ballistic missile technology.
  • Served as the Project Director of India's first Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III) which successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in near-earth orbit.
  • Served as the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and Secretary of the Defense Research and Development Organisation (DRDO).
  • Served as the 11th Presideent of India from 2002 to 2007, known as the 'People's President'.
  • Philosophy and Vision
  • Advocated for India's self-reliance in defense technology and space exploration.
  • Emphasized the importance of education, particularly in science and technology, to propel India's development.
  • Often spoke about the need for young people to dream big and work hard to achieve their goals.
  • Achievements
  • Awarded the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian honor, in 1997 for his contributions to science and technology.
  • Received several other awards and honors, both nationally and internationally, for his work in space and missile technology.
  • Authored several books including "Wings of Fire" which is an autobiography and "Ignited Minds" which focuses on igniting the minds of Indian youth.
  • Legacy
  • Fondly remembered as the "Missile Man of India" for his contributions to India's missile technology.
  • Known for his humility, simplicity, and dedication to science and education.
  • Continues to inspire millions of Indians, especially youth, to pursue their dreams with determination and hard work.
